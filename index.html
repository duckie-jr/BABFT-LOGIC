Inputs & Combining Gates

Real game objects, timing, and building circuits with more than 2 inputs.

Levers & Switches

Levers and switches are your signal sources. Connect their output wire directly into any gate input. That's it — no extra setup needed.

1 Lever ON — active signal
0 Lever OFF — no signal

Delay Block

Passes a signal through after a fixed wait. Set anywhere between 0.05s and 10s. Useful for timing sequences, preventing rapid toggling, or building a simple clock pulse.

3+ Input Gates

Every gate in BaBFT takes exactly 2 inputs. To AND three signals together, chain two AND gates — first gate handles A & B, second gate handles that result & C.

Inverting a Signal (NOT)

There is no NOT gate. Use a NOR gate with one input permanently wired to 0 — in-game, leave that input disconnected or tie it to a lever that is always off.

Clock / Oscillator

A single NOR gate looped through a delay — generates a repeating pulse with no external input.

Feed the output of a NOR gate back into one of its own inputs through a delay block. Tie the second input permanently to 0 so the gate acts as a NOT. The delay stops the feedback from resolving instantly — giving the output time to flip before the new value arrives back, which flips it again, and so on forever.

  • Gate rule: NOR(0, signal) = NOT(signal) — output is always the inverse of the feedback input
  • Delay role: breaks the instantaneous loop — the output holds for one delay tick before the feedback catches up
  • Result: CLK toggles 0→1→0→1 automatically, no lever needed
  • Speed: shorter delay = faster clock; longer delay = slower clock

Half Adder

Your first real circuit — adds two 1-bit numbers using one XOR and one AND gate.

A half adder takes two inputs and produces two outputs. Connect two levers, wire both into a XOR gate and an AND gate in parallel.

  • Sum = XOR(A, B) — is 1 when exactly one input is 1
  • Carry = AND(A, B) — is 1 only when both are 1, meaning the result overflowed into the next bit

2-to-4 Decoder

2 input bits select exactly 1 of 4 output lines. The building block of any address-based circuit.

A decoder reads a binary number and turns on one specific output. With 2 bits you have 4 possible values (00, 01, 10, 11) so you get 4 outputs — only one is ever active at a time.

Each output is an AND gate wired to the right mix of A, B, and their inverses. The inverses come from NOR(x, 0) — BaBFT's only way to invert.

Scaling up in BaBFT

A full 2-to-4 decoder costs 2 NOR gates (inverters) + 4 AND gates = 6 gates total. To address 30 pixels you need a 5-bit decoder — 5 NOR gates for the inverters, then up to 32 AND chains (one per address), each AND chain being 4 two-input AND gates deep. That's around 130 gates just for the address logic.

SR Latch

Two cross-coupled NOR gates — gives your circuit memory.

An SR latch stores one bit of state. The output Q stays 1 or 0 even after you remove the input signal — it remembers. In BaBFT this means a door that stays open after a button press, a trap that arms once and waits, or any mechanism that needs to "latch on."

  • S = 1, R = 0 — Set: Q goes to 1 and stays there
  • S = 0, R = 1 — Reset: Q goes to 0 and stays there
  • S = 0, R = 0 — Hold: Q keeps whatever it was last
  • S = 1, R = 1 — Invalid: avoid this, both outputs fight

Toggle Latch (T-Latch)

Press once — ON. Press again — OFF. One button, persistent state, zero levers left on.

A toggle latch flips its output every time the trigger input T pulses. Built from the SR latch — the circuit automatically picks Set or Reset so you never have to think about it.

What is Q̅ (Q-bar)? The SR latch produces two outputs: Q (the main output, bottom NOR gate) and Q̅ (top NOR gate — always the reverse of Q). When Q=0 then Q̅=1, and vice versa. You get it for free from the SR latch’s cross-coupling. In the diagram below it is the upper dashed wire looping back over the top.
  • T = 0 (idle): both AND gates output 0 → SR latch holds — Q unchanged
  • T = 1, Q = 0: AND_S fires → S=1, R=0 → SR sets → Q becomes 1
  • T = 1, Q = 1: AND_R fires → S=0, R=1 → SR resets → Q becomes 0
  • Use a button (momentary), not a lever — a lever held ON will keep toggling every delay tick
  • 4 gates total: 2 AND + 2 NOR (the SR latch)

2-to-1 Multiplexer

A selector bit chooses which of two signals reaches the output.

A MUX is a programmable signal switch. One lever (Sel) controls which source gets through — flip it one way and signal A passes, flip it the other way and signal B passes. The output wire stays the same; only the source changes.

  • Sel = 0 → Out = A (B is blocked)
  • Sel = 1 → Out = B (A is blocked)
  • 4 gates total: 1 NOR (inverts Sel) + 2 AND (one per path) + 1 OR (combines)

Full Adder

Adds two bits plus a carry-in — chain these to build multi-bit addition.

A full adder is two half adders wired together with an OR gate. The carry-in lets you chain stages — the carry-out of one full adder feeds the carry-in of the next.

  • Stage 1: S1 = XOR(A, B)    C1 = AND(A, B)
  • Stage 2: Sum = XOR(S1, Cin)    C2 = AND(S1, Cin)
  • Cout = OR(C1, C2) — carry-out to the next stage
  • 5 gates total: 2 XOR + 2 AND + 1 OR

4-bit Ripple-Carry Adder

Chain 4 full adders — carry ripples from bit 0 up to bit 3, adding two 4-bit numbers into a 5-bit result.

Every full adder handles one bit position. The carry-out of FA₀ feeds the carry-in of FA₁ — it must settle before FA₁ can produce its output, which is why the propagation delay "ripples" upward through all four stages.

  • FA₀ (bit 0 — LSB): Cin is always tied to 0
  • FA₁–FA₃: each Cin comes from the carry-out of the stage below
  • Cout (bit 4): overflow — sum exceeds 1111₂ (15)
  • Range: 0 + 0 = 0 up to 15 + 15 = 30 (11110₂)
  • Gate cost: 4 × 5 = 20 gates (8 XOR + 8 AND + 4 OR)

Two-Digit Segment Display

12 LED screens in additive mode — two digits sharing inner verticals I and M to cut gate cost.

Each of the 12 segments is a BaBFT LED screen set to additive mode, white colour, gated through an AND gate from a segment decoder. The inner verticals I and M sit physically between the digits — because additive screens naturally combine any signal driving them, both digits can activate a shared segment simultaneously at zero extra gate cost.

  • Digit 1 uses: A1, H, I (shared), G1, L, M (shared), D1
  • Digit 2 uses: A2, I (shared), K, G2, M (shared), N, D2
  • Additive OR for free: if both digits need I or M lit, the screen just stays on — no extra gate
  • Gate cost: 2× 4-to-16 decoders + ~7 OR chains per digit feeding segment AND gates
  • Cool use: wire the 4-bit adder output directly — display 0–30 as two decimal digits

LED Display System

Minimum build: 3 color levers, 1 activate lever, 3 AND gates, 3 delay blocks, and a screen. Colors mix additively — more channels active = brighter compound color.

How it works

Each color channel (R, G, B) has its own AND gate. The AND gate passes the color only when the Activate lever is also ON. Each gated signal then flows through a Delay block before reaching the screen channel. The screen mixes additively — more channels = brighter, different color.

  • AND gate: AND(color lever, activate) — gates each channel individually
  • Delay: conditions the signal before it hits the screen, prevents instant flicker
  • Screen (additive): R+G = Yellow, R+B = Magenta, G+B = Cyan, R+G+B = White
